DIGITIZATION

Transform physical collections to accessible data. This resource highlights the primary Benefits, Challenges, and Tips for successful preservation. It offers an overview to establishing a solid foundation in digital archiving.

What is Digitization?

Digitizaiton is the process of tranlating phsyical text, pictures, or artifacts (or anything!) into digital form.

The Main Challenges:

  • Maintaining the authenticity of the original item

  • Organizing the data and gathering metadata

  • Automating the process & handling a large amount of data

How to Approach Digitization:

  • Plan and prioritize what to digitize

  • Use consistent equipment settings and file formats

  • Add clear filenames and metadata

  • Store and back up files safely

  • Review and update your system over time

Essay:

The Digital Mindset

Digitization is more than just a technical task; it is a form of translation. When we move objects from the physical shelf to the digital cloud, we aren't just copying them, we are deciding what is worth saving. The goal isn't to replace the physical artifact, but to create a digital surrogate that allows people to access history from anywhere in the world. This accessibility bridges the gap between physical preservation and public education, allowing users who cannot travel to the archive to study the materials.

Curation over Accumulation

A common mistake is to try to "scan everything" immediately. Effective digitization requires curation. Ask yourself: What items are most at risk of physical decay? What collections are in high demand by researchers? By prioritizing these items, you turn a massive backlog into a manageable project that delivers immediate value to your audience. Strategic selection ensures that your limited resources are spent on the materials that tell the most important stories, preventing burnout and inconsistent quality.

Data is the Key

Finally, remember that a digital image without data is just a pretty picture. If you cannot search for it, you cannot find it. Investing time in robust metadata—tagging dates, creators, and descriptions is just as important as the quality of the scan itself. This ensures your digital collection remains usable and "alive" for the future. Think of metadata as the map that helps future users navigate your digital library; without it, your files are just digital clutter.

Consistency Counts

Whether you are scanning documents or photographing artifacts, keeping your settings uniform is vital. If one image is high-resolution and the next is blurry, the collection loses professionalism and utility. Establish a clear workflow and stick to it, ensuring that every file meets the same standard of quality and format. This discipline makes the collection easier to manage and much more pleasant for the user to browse.

Planning for Preservation

Digitization is not a one-time event; it is an ongoing commitment to maintenance. Hard drives fail and file formats become obsolete, so having a backup strategy is non-negotiable. Always follow the rule of redundancy: keep multiple copies of your data in different locations to ensure your work survives the test of time. If you do not plan for the long term, you risk losing the very history you worked so hard to save.
